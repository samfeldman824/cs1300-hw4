Public Microwave Usability Study

Exploring the user experience of communal microwaves

Why Study Microwaves?

Microwaves in shared spaces—offices, dorms, and break rooms—are everywhere, yet their interfaces often confuse users. Cryptic symbols, unresponsive buttons, and minimal feedback create frustration. This study aims to uncover those pain points and recommend design improvements.

Study Context at CIT

The study was conducted in the communal kitchens within the Computing and Information Technology (CIT) building at Brown University. Over two full days during lunch hours and mid-afternoon breaks, I observed users as they interacted with countertop microwaves. I recorded each interaction—button sequences, preset usage, and session durations—while taking care to remain unobtrusive. Immediately following their use, I conducted 12 brief, informal interviews to capture real-time feedback on clarity, ease of use, and perceived responsiveness.

Initial Observations

  • The vast majority of users entered cooking time via the numeric keypad and pressed “Start,” bypassing presets, power-level adjustments, and other buttons.
  • Several users waited close to the microwave, watching the digital display for confirmation before stepping away.
  • A few pressed the “Add 30s” button repeatedly, expecting it to function like a quick-increment feature.
  • Some users opened the door mid-cycle to verify operation, inadvertently pausing the heating process.
  • Variations in keypad press speed suggested uncertainty about whether inputs were registered.

User Interviews

1. How do you typically set cooking time?
Most users enter the exact time on the numeric keypad and press “Start” for precision, while some rely on quick presets (like a 1-minute button) when hurried.
2. Do you use the power-level or defrost features?
Interviewees rarely use these options; those who tried them found the labels unclear and preferred cooking at full power.
3. Have you used the “Add 30s” quick button?
The “Add 30s” feature is appreciated for its convenience, though a few participants were unaware it existed.
4. What frustrates you most about this microwave?
Users mentioned sticky or unresponsive buttons, ambiguous beeps, and lack of visible progress feedback as their main pain points.
5. What do you find most helpful?
They praised the clear numeric display, the intuitive Start button, and the quick-presets for streamlining interactions.
6. What improvements would you suggest?
Common suggestions included adding a visual progress bar, enlarging and spacing buttons for better responsiveness, and a memory function for last-used settings.

What is an Annotated Sketch?

An annotated sketch visually breaks down the interface by labeling key components and interactions. It highlights elements like the keypad, display, presets, and door handle to clarify their functions and guide design improvements.

Understanding the Personas

Before diving into the empathy map visuals, it’s important to understand why we use personas. These images represent two archetypal users—a Busy Student and an Office Professional—capturing their goals, frustrations, and behaviors when using a microwave. By mapping what they say, think, do, and feel, we gain deeper insights into user needs.

What is a Storyboard?

A storyboard is a visual sequence of frames illustrating key moments in a user's interaction with a product. It helps communicate the flow of actions, emotions, and touchpoints in a clear narrative.
